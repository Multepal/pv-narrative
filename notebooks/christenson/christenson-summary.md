**Quiché-Maya Historical Context**

The Quiché-Maya, a branch of the Maya civilization, dominated western Guatemala before the Spanish conquest in 1524. Their history is intertwined with the Popol Vuh, a sacred text that reflects their cultural and political influences, particularly from central Mexico.
- The Quiché-Maya population exceeds half a million, primarily in Quiché, Totonicapán, and Quetzaltenango.
- The Popol Vuh was authored by Quiché nobility and suggests a history of militaristic dominance by the Cavec-Quiché lineage.
- The highland Maya were influenced by Nahua speakers and central Mexican cultures, particularly during the Postclassic period (AD 900-1200).
- Tikal and Chichen Itza were significant centers of Mexican influence, with Chichen Itza being linked to the legendary Kukulcan.

**Influence of Central Mexican Cultures**

- The arrival of Teotihuacan warriors in AD 378 marked the beginning of significant Mexican influence in the Maya region.
- Chichen Itza served as a major center for this influence, with its architecture and ceremonial practices reflecting Toltec traditions.
- The Quiché lineage claimed Toltec ancestry, emphasizing their connection to central Mexican culture and legitimizing their rule.
